When you pull into an Australian service station, you are greeted by a row of nozzles labelled with codes like E10, U91, U95, U98, Diesel and LPG. Each represents a different type of fuel with distinct properties, price points and engine compatibility. Choosing the right one matters for your engine's health, your wallet and sometimes the environment.

## Unleaded 91 (U91)

U91 is the baseline unleaded petrol in Australia. The number 91 refers to its Research Octane Number (RON), which measures resistance to engine knock. Most passenger cars manufactured before 2005 and many budget models run perfectly well on U91. It is almost always the cheapest petrol option at the bowser.

## Ethanol Blend E10

E10 is unleaded petrol blended with up to 10 per cent ethanol derived from sugar cane or grain. It typically carries a RON of 94 and costs a few cents less per litre than U91. Not every car is compatible with E10 — check your owner's manual or the fuel flap sticker. If your car accepts it, E10 is often the most cost-effective choice.

## Premium 95 (U95)

Premium 95 has a higher octane rating than U91, which means it resists pre-ignition (knocking) better under load. Many modern turbocharged four-cylinder engines, European cars and some Japanese imports require a minimum of 95 RON. Using a lower-octane fuel in these engines can trigger the knock sensor and reduce performance.

## Premium 98 (U98)

U98 is the highest-octane petrol widely available in Australia. High-performance and luxury vehicles often specify 98 RON for optimal power delivery and efficiency. While it costs the most per litre, drivers of compatible cars may see slightly better fuel economy because the engine can advance timing further.

## Diesel

Diesel fuel powers compression-ignition engines common in SUVs, utes, trucks and some passenger cars. Diesel engines are more thermally efficient than petrol engines, meaning they extract more energy per litre, which translates to longer range. However, diesel has been more expensive than petrol in recent years due to refining costs and global demand.

## Premium Diesel

Some stations sell premium diesel with added detergent and cetane-boosting additives. The higher cetane number means smoother combustion and potentially better cold-start behaviour. Whether the extra cost is worthwhile depends on your driving style and engine age.

## LPG (Liquefied Petroleum Gas)

LPG is a mix of propane and butane stored under moderate pressure. It burns cleaner than petrol with lower carbon dioxide emissions. The trade-off is roughly 15 to 20 per cent lower energy density, so you use more litres per hundred kilometres. LPG is significantly cheaper per litre, which often offsets the higher consumption. Fewer new cars come with factory LPG systems today, but many older Falcons and Commodores have aftermarket conversions.

## Which Fuel Should You Use?

Always follow the minimum octane rating specified by your vehicle manufacturer. Using a higher octane than required will not damage your engine but usually provides no measurable benefit in a standard car. Using a lower octane than required can cause knocking, reduced power and potential engine damage over time.

Check the fuel flap or owner's manual to confirm your car's requirements before filling up.
