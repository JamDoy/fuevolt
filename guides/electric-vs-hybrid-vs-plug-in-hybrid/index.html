Electric vs Hybrid vs Plug-in Hybrid: Which Is Right for You?

By James Doyle · Comparison · 6 min read

Last updated: 19 July 2026

Compare pure electric (BEV), hybrid (HEV) and plug-in hybrid (PHEV) vehicles to find the best fit for your driving needs and budget.

Walk into a dealership today and you will hear "hybrid", "self-charging hybrid", "plug-in hybrid" and "EV" thrown around almost interchangeably — and it is genuinely confusing. It is easy to buy a "hybrid" expecting to plug it in, only to discover that it never plugs in at all. Getting the terminology straight before you shop saves a lot of that frustration.

There are real, practical differences between an HEV, a PHEV and a full battery EV, and each suits a different kind of driver. The government's Green Vehicle Guide explains how each hybrid and electric type is classified, which is a useful reference as you compare models.

Hybrid Electric Vehicle (HEV)

A hybrid combines a petrol engine with a small electric motor and battery. The battery charges itself through regenerative braking and the petrol engine — you never plug it in. The electric motor assists during acceleration and can power the car at low speeds for short distances.

Popular examples include the Toyota Corolla Hybrid, RAV4 Hybrid and Honda CR-V Hybrid. Fuel consumption is typically 30 to 40 per cent lower than the equivalent petrol-only model.

Hybrids are the simplest transition from a conventional car. There is no charging infrastructure to think about, no range anxiety and no change to your fuelling habits. You just use less petrol.

Plug-in Hybrid Electric Vehicle (PHEV)

A plug-in hybrid has a larger battery than a standard hybrid, typically offering 40 to 80 kilometres of pure electric range. You charge the battery from a power point or wall box at home, drive on electric power for your daily commute, and the petrol engine kicks in for longer trips.

Popular PHEVs in Australia include the Mitsubishi Outlander PHEV, BYD Sealion 6 and MG HS Plus EV. When driven mostly on electric power with regular charging, a PHEV can achieve fuel consumption figures below 2 litres per 100 km.

However, if you rarely charge the battery and rely on the petrol engine, a PHEV is actually less efficient than a standard hybrid because it carries the extra weight of a larger battery and electric motor. PHEVs only make financial sense if you commit to plugging in regularly.

Battery Electric Vehicle (BEV)

A battery electric vehicle has no petrol engine at all. It runs entirely on electric power stored in a large battery pack. Range varies from 300 km for entry-level models to over 600 km for premium versions.

BEVs have the lowest running costs — no petrol, minimal servicing and cheap home charging. They also produce zero tailpipe emissions. The trade-offs are higher purchase price, dependence on charging infrastructure for long trips and longer refuelling times compared to petrol.

Cost Comparison

For a driver covering 15,000 km per year:

A petrol car at 8 L/100 km costs roughly $2,040 in fuel. A hybrid at 5 L/100 km costs roughly $1,275 in fuel. A PHEV driven 70 per cent electric costs roughly $500 in electricity plus $380 in petrol ($880 total). A BEV at 16 kWh/100 km costs roughly $600 in electricity (home charging).

Servicing costs follow a similar pattern — BEVs are cheapest, hybrids and PHEVs are comparable to petrol, and all are cheaper than diesel.

Which One Is Right for You?

Choose a hybrid if you want fuel savings with zero lifestyle change and a lower purchase price.

Choose a plug-in hybrid if you have a short daily commute (under 60 km), reliable home charging, and occasionally need to drive long distances without planning charging stops.

Choose a battery electric vehicle if you can charge at home, want the lowest possible running costs, and are comfortable planning charging stops on longer trips.

Consider a diesel only if you tow heavy loads regularly or drive very high kilometres annually in a vehicle class where EV options are limited (large utes and heavy-duty SUVs).

The Trend

The direction of the market is clear — according to the Electric Vehicle Council, EV uptake in Australia continues to climb even as the country works through charging and policy challenges. BEV sales are growing fastest, PHEV sales are steady, and hybrid technology is becoming standard across most model ranges. Whatever you choose today will serve you well, but the future is increasingly electric.
